A knowledge graph is a network of connected concepts, facts, and relationships. Unlike a flat document store, a knowledge graph lets you navigate ideas by connection rather than by search.

## Why Knowledge Graphs Matter

Most note-taking systems store notes in isolation. A knowledge graph:
- Reveals **unexpected connections** between ideas
- Shows which concepts are **central** to your thinking
- Identifies **gaps** in your understanding
- Makes **retrieval by association** natural

## The Zettelkasten Method

Zettelkasten (German for "slip box") is the original knowledge graph. Created by sociologist Niklas Luhmann, who produced 90 books and 400 papers using a system of 90,000 index cards linked by references.

Key principles:
1. **Atomic notes** — one idea per note
2. **Explicit links** — connect notes when they relate
3. **Emergence** — structure emerges from connections, not top-down hierarchy

## Automatic vs Manual Linking

Manual linking (Obsidian [[wikilinks]]) requires discipline. meshblog's approach: **automatic entity extraction** creates links without user effort.

When you write about React and TypeScript in the same note, the system automatically creates a relationship between those entities. Over hundreds of notes, a rich graph emerges.

## From Graph to Insight

The knowledge graph enables:
- **Concept clustering** (Louvain): grouping related entities into themes
- **Centrality scoring** (PageRank): finding your most-connected ideas
- **FAQ generation**: turning dense connections into navigable questions
